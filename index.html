Ancient Indian Knowledge System

India's scholars developed sophisticated systems of mathematics, astronomy, and timekeeping millennia ahead of their time. AryaVidya preserves two: Pingala's binary arithmetic and the Vedic Panchanga calendar.

📿
Chandahshastra — The Binary System
Pingala (~300 BCE) described the binary number system in his Sanskrit prosody treatise using laghu (light, 0) and guru (heavy, 1) syllables to encode verse patterns. His Nasta and Uddhista algorithms are precise inverse operations, predating Leibniz by nearly 2,000 years.
🌙
Panchanga — The Five Limbs of Time
The ancient Hindu almanac. Its five elements — Tithi, Vara, Naksatra, Yoga, and Karana — precisely define every moment in Vedic timekeeping. Still consulted daily across India for ceremonies, marriages, travel, and planting.
☽
Tithi — The Lunar Day
A Tithi is 1/30th of a lunar month — the time for the Moon to gain 12° on the Sun. There are 30 Tithis: 15 in the bright fortnight (Shukla Paksha) ending with Purnima, and 15 in the dark fortnight (Krishna Paksha) ending with Amavasya. Each lasts ~23.6 hours.
✦
Karana — Half a Lunar Day
A Karana is half a Tithi (6° of Moon-Sun elongation). There are 11 Karanas: 4 fixed (Kimstughna, Shakuni, Chatushpada, Naga) and 7 repeating (Bava, Balava, Kaulava, Taitila, Gara, Vanija, Vishti) cycling 8 times through the month.
⭐
Naksatra — The 27 Lunar Mansions
The 27 Naksatras are star clusters through which the Moon passes, each spanning 800 arc-minutes (13°20') from Ashwini to Revati. Used in Jyotisha for birth charts, muhurta, and compatibility readings based on the Moon's ~27.3-day sidereal period.
🕉
Yoga — Combined Lunar-Solar Interval
A Yoga is found by adding Moon and Sun longitudes, divided into 27 equal parts of 800 arc-minutes each. The 27 Yogas from Vishkambha to Vaidhriti range from auspicious (Siddhi, Shubha) to inauspicious (Vishkambha, Vyatipata). Essential for muhurta calculations.
🔢
Vedic Mathematics & Astronomy
The Sulba Sutras (~800–200 BCE) state the Pythagorean theorem. Aryabhata (~499 CE) calculated π to 3.14159 and the sidereal year to within 3 minutes. Brahmagupta (628 CE) established arithmetic with zero. The Kerala school (14th–16th century) developed infinite series predating Newton and Leibniz.
